What is an EAD and how do you get one?

An Employment Authorization Document (EAD), also called Form I-766, is a card issued by USCIS that proves you are authorized to work in the United States. You apply using Form I-765. Not everyone needs one—some visa holders (H-1B, L-1, TN) are work-authorized by their status, not a separate card.

Applies to: General Verified April 21, 2026

Who this applies to

This page provides a general overview of the Employment Authorization Document (EAD) — what it is, who needs one, and how to apply. Specific EAD categories (such as OPT for F-1 students, H-4 EAD for H-4 spouses, or DACA EAD) have their own pages with more detailed information.

What the law says

Under 8 CFR § 274a.12, all noncitizens who are not U.S. citizens or lawful permanent residents must either be authorized to work by their nonimmigrant status or obtain an EAD. The regulation lists dozens of categories — divided into three groups:

8 CFR § 274a.12(a) — Aliens authorized incident to status (no application needed): These workers are authorized to work solely by virtue of their immigration status. Examples include lawful permanent residents (green card holders), refugees, and asylees.

8 CFR § 274a.12(b) — Aliens authorized for specific employment: These workers are authorized only to work for a specific employer. H-1B, L-1, O-1, and TN visa holders fall in this category. Their authorization comes from their approved petition or admission — no EAD is needed.

8 CFR § 274a.12(c) — Aliens who must apply for work authorization: These workers must apply to USCIS using Form I-765. Examples include F-1 OPT students, H-4 EAD applicants, DACA recipients, asylum applicants, and many others.

How to apply (Form I-765):

  1. Determine your category code (listed in the Form I-765 instructions; e.g., “(c)(3)(B)” for OPT, “(c)(26)” for H-4 EAD)
  2. Complete Form I-765 with supporting documents specific to your category
  3. Pay the filing fee (fee waivers available for certain categories)
  4. Mail or file electronically (online filing available for some categories)
  5. Receive your EAD card by mail — do not begin work before receiving the card and before your authorized start date

What the EAD looks like:
The EAD (Form I-766) is a credit-card-sized document with your photo, name, alien registration number, category, and the card’s validity dates. It is a List A document for Form I-9 employment eligibility verification — it establishes both identity and work authorization simultaneously.

Duration:
EAD validity periods vary by category, typically 1–2 years. Renewal applications should be filed well before expiration (USCIS recommends filing at least 180 days in advance for most categories).

Common gotchas

  • The card, not the approval notice, is what matters for I-9 purposes. You cannot present an EAD approval notice to an employer for I-9. You need the physical card.
  • Not every work-authorized person needs an EAD. H-1B workers, L-1 workers, TN professionals, and O-1 workers use their visa/status documents for I-9 purposes. Obtaining an EAD they don’t need can actually cause confusion.
  • EADs are category-specific. An EAD issued under the OPT category does not extend to cover work in a different category. If your status changes, you may need a new EAD under the new category.
  • An expired EAD does not authorize work. Even if a renewal is pending, once the current EAD expires you cannot work unless USCIS issues an automatic extension (available for some categories when the renewal is filed on time).
  • Automatic extension rules vary by category. Some EAD categories qualify for a 180-day automatic extension if renewed on time. Others do not. Check the USCIS automatic extension chart for your specific category.
  • REAL ID compliance: EADs are accepted by TSA for domestic air travel as a valid ID.
