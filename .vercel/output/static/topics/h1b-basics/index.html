What can H-1B workers do and not do?

H-1B workers are authorized to work only for the specific employer that petitioned for them, in the specific specialty occupation listed in the approved petition. You cannot work for a different employer or in a different occupation without a new H-1B petition (or AC21 portability rights after 180 days of a pending green card application).

Applies to: H-1B Verified April 21, 2026

Who this applies to

This page covers workers who currently hold H-1B nonimmigrant status in the United States. It also applies to workers awaiting an H-1B petition decision. If you are an H-1B holder considering additional work, changing employers, or switching occupations, this page explains the rules.

What the law says

Under 8 CFR § 214.2(h) and INA § 101(a)(15)(H), the H-1B classification is for workers in a specialty occupation — defined as an occupation requiring the theoretical and practical application of a body of highly specialized knowledge, and attainment of a bachelor’s degree or higher (or its equivalent) in the specific specialty.

What H-1B workers are authorized to do:

  • Work for the petitioning employer in the approved specialty occupation, at the approved worksite(s)
  • Work for multiple H-1B employers simultaneously if each employer has filed a separate H-1B petition and each is approved
  • Travel internationally and re-enter on a valid H-1B visa
  • Pursue education while maintaining H-1B status (with limitations)

What H-1B workers are not authorized to do:

  • Work for an employer who has not filed an approved or pending H-1B petition on their behalf
  • Work in an occupation not covered by the approved LCA and petition (e.g., an IT consultant who takes on accounting work for a different employer)
  • Freelance, consult independently, or start a business without a properly structured H-1B arrangement (see the side work page)

Duration:
Initial H-1B approval is typically for 3 years, extendable to 6 years total. Further extensions beyond 6 years may be available under the American Competitiveness in the Twenty-first Century Act (AC21) if an I-140 immigrant petition has been approved for 365 days or more or a PERM labor certification has been pending for 365 days or more.

Changing employers:
If you change jobs, your new employer must file a new H-1B petition before you begin work for them (with limited exceptions). Under 8 CFR § 214.2(h)(2)(i)(H), if you are a cap-exempt transfer (switching to a new petitioner, not needing to go through the cap again), you may begin work as soon as the new employer files — you do not need to wait for approval under the H-1B portability rules. Confirm timing with an attorney.

Common gotchas

  • Moving worksites without updating the LCA can violate your status. The Labor Condition Application (LCA) filed with the Department of Labor lists specific worksites. If your employer moves you to a different location — or if you start working remotely from a different state — the LCA may need to be amended before you can work from the new location.
  • Short-term placements at third-party sites have special rules. For H-1B workers placed at third-party client sites (common in IT consulting), USCIS scrutinizes whether a valid employer-employee relationship exists with the H-1B petitioner.
  • 180-day I-485 portability. If you have an I-485 (green card application) that has been pending for 180 days or more, and an approved I-140, you may be able to change jobs and retain your priority date under AC21 — even if the new job is at a different employer in the same or similar occupational classification. This requires careful legal analysis.
  • Your status is tied to your employer’s actions. If your employer withdraws the H-1B petition or goes out of business, you immediately lose H-1B authorization. You have a 60-day grace period after involuntary termination to find new employer sponsorship, change status, or depart the US.
  • H-1B status does not automatically extend while a renewal is pending. Work with your employer to file renewals well in advance.

What this page does not cover

  • Freelance and side work — whether H-1B holders can do independent consulting or moonlighting
