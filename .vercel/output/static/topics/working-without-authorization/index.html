What happens if you work without authorization?

Working without authorization is a serious violation of U.S. immigration law. Consequences can include deportation, multi-year bars on returning to the United States, denial of future visas and green cards, and criminal prosecution for employers. There is no statute of limitations—past unauthorized work can affect applications years later.

Applies to: General Verified April 21, 2026

Who this applies to

This page is relevant to any noncitizen in the United States who is working — or considering working — without valid work authorization. It is also relevant to noncitizens who are unsure whether their status permits a particular type of work.

What the law says

Under INA § 274A, it is unlawful for any person to work in the United States knowing they are not authorized to do so, and unlawful for any employer to hire, recruit, or refer for a fee any noncitizen knowing they are unauthorized.

Consequences for the worker:

  • Status violation: Unauthorized employment is a per se violation of nonimmigrant status conditions. For most visa categories, any unauthorized work — even one day — terminates your valid status immediately.
  • Unlawful presence: Once you lose status, you begin accruing unlawful presence. Under INA § 212(a)(9)(B):
    • 180 days to less than 1 year of unlawful presence → 3-year bar on reentry after departure
    • 1 year or more of unlawful presence → 10-year bar on reentry after departure
    • Certain waivers exist but are difficult to obtain
  • Removal proceedings: USCIS or ICE can initiate removal (deportation) proceedings for status violations, including unauthorized employment.
  • Future visa and green card denials: Unauthorized employment is a ground of inadmissibility and can be used to deny future visa applications, green card petitions, and adjustment of status applications.
  • No protection for good faith. Believing you were authorized — even if told so by an employer or DSO — is generally not a defense to a status violation if you were in fact not authorized.

Consequences for employers:

Under INA § 274A, employers who knowingly hire unauthorized workers face:

  • Civil fines of $250–$2,000 per unauthorized worker for a first offense; higher for repeat violations
  • Criminal prosecution for pattern or practice violations (up to 6 months imprisonment and $3,000 per worker)
  • Debarment from federal contracts

Special note for F-1 students on OPT: A related (but different) issue for F-1 students is the 90-day unemployment rule — see the OPT unemployment page. The unemployment limit is a specific form of status violation tied to OPT.

Common gotchas

  • “Volunteer” work can count as unauthorized employment if it primarily benefits a for-profit employer and displaces regular workers. See volunteering vs. work for details.
  • Offshore or remote work for a foreign company generally does not constitute “employment in the United States” if the work is performed from outside the US. But if performed from within the US, it may constitute work in the US regardless of who pays you or where the company is based.
  • Being paid in cash, crypto, or foreign currency does not change the rule. The medium of payment is irrelevant.
  • Work performed before your status was violated can still be used against you in later immigration proceedings. Unauthorized work from years ago can appear on background checks and affect green card or visa applications.
  • Employers can be liable even without knowledge in some circumstances if they failed to properly complete Form I-9 verification.
  • Tolling of unlawful presence. For some visa categories (including F and J status), unlawful presence does not begin until USCIS issues a formal finding of violation or an immigration judge orders removal — it does not begin the moment status is violated. However, this is a technical distinction and should not be relied upon without legal advice.
