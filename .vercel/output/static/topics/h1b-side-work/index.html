Can H-1B workers do freelance or side work?

No—not without a separate H-1B petition. H-1B status authorizes you to work only for the specific employer that petitioned for you. Any freelance, consulting, or second job requires that employer to file its own H-1B petition. Self-employment is similarly restricted.

Applies to: H-1B Verified April 21, 2026

Who this applies to

This page is for H-1B workers who want to take on additional work outside their primary H-1B employer — for example, freelance consulting, part-time work at a second company, teaching a course, speaking fees, or work through their own company.

What the law says

Under INA § 101(a)(15)(H) and 8 CFR § 214.2(h), H-1B status is granted to work for a specific petitioning employer. The authorization is employer-specific, not occupation-specific in terms of who can pay you.

Concurrent H-1B employment is allowed, but only if each employer files its own separate H-1B petition. USCIS calls this “concurrent employment.” Key points:

  • Each employer must independently file Form I-129 and (for non-cap-exempt employers) go through the cap or claim an exemption
  • The new employer’s petition must be approved (or at minimum pending, under H-1B portability rules) before you begin working for them
  • Each employer files its own LCA with the Department of Labor
  • There is no limit on how many concurrent H-1B employers you can have — but each requires its own approved petition

Self-employment: Starting your own company and having it petition for you in H-1B is theoretically possible, but extremely difficult in practice. USCIS requires evidence of a genuine employer-employee relationship — the petitioning entity must have the right to control your work. A sole-owner of a company generally cannot be both the employer and the H-1B employee because USCIS does not recognize that level of self-control as a legitimate employer-employee relationship. Some structures (e.g., a company with a board of directors or multiple owners) have succeeded, but these arrangements require careful legal planning.

Passive income is generally fine. Income from investments, rental properties, book royalties, or similar passive sources does not constitute “employment” under immigration law. You can generally earn passive income without a separate H-1B petition.

Honoraria and speaking fees occupy a gray area. USCIS has historically allowed very limited payments for isolated, incidental activities (a single lecture, attending a professional conference). However, recurring consulting work, even if labeled as “honoraria,” is employment and requires a petition.

Common gotchas

  • Working for a client directly, even informally, is unauthorized employment. Even one hour of paid consulting work for an entity that has not filed an H-1B petition on your behalf is unauthorized employment. There is no de minimis exception.
  • “Working for free” for a for-profit company can still be unauthorized. If the work primarily benefits the company rather than you, and would displace a paid worker, it may be treated as unauthorized employment. See volunteering vs. work.
  • Teaching a single course at a university may be exempt. Cap-exempt organizations (nonprofits, universities, government research organizations) may file H-1B petitions and are not subject to the annual cap — this can make it easier to add a teaching position.
  • Do not start side work “just to try it out.” Even a brief unauthorized work period creates a status violation that can affect future visa petitions, green card applications, and admissibility.
  • Income reported on a K-1 from a partnership in which you are a material participant may be treated as wages. Consult a tax attorney about business structures and immigration implications together.
