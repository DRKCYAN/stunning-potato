What is CPT and when can F-1 students use it?

Curricular Practical Training (CPT) lets F-1 students work off campus in jobs that are a required or integral part of their academic curriculum. Your school's DSO must authorize it in SEVIS before you start—there is no USCIS filing required. Full-time CPT for 12 months or more eliminates all OPT eligibility.

Applies to: F-1 Verified April 21, 2026

Who this applies to

This page applies to F-1 students who want to work off campus as part of their academic program. CPT applies when the work experience is a required or integral part of your established curriculum — such as a required internship course, cooperative education (co-op) program, or practicum.

What the law says

Under 8 CFR § 214.2(f)(10)(i), CPT is defined as alternative work/study, internship, cooperative education, or any other type of required internship or practicum that is offered by sponsoring employers through cooperative agreements with the school.

Key requirements under the regulation:

  • The training must be an integral part of an established curriculum. This means the course offering CPT credit must exist in the school’s catalog and the work must be directly tied to the student’s major area of study.
  • One academic year of full-time study is required before a student may begin CPT — unless the graduate program requires immediate curricular training as part of the program design.
  • DSO authorization is required in SEVIS before you may begin working. Your DSO issues a new I-20 with the CPT authorization endorsed on it. You must have the updated I-20 before the start date.
  • Part-time CPT means 20 hours or fewer per week. You may hold part-time CPT during the academic year while enrolled full time.
  • Full-time CPT means more than 20 hours per week. Generally requires school approval and may count against your OPT.

The OPT penalty rule: If you use full-time CPT for 12 months or more in the aggregate, you become ineligible for OPT entirely. Part-time CPT has no effect on OPT eligibility regardless of duration.

Common gotchas

  • “Integral to the curriculum” has teeth. Simply enrolling in an independent study course and asking your DSO to approve CPT is not sufficient if the program does not genuinely require practical training. Schools that approve CPT improperly can lose SEVP certification, and students can face status violations.
  • Each CPT authorization is employer-specific and time-limited. If you change employers or extend the end date, your DSO must issue a new I-20 with updated CPT authorization before you begin with the new employer or continue past the original end date.
  • No OPT safety net if you use 12 months of full-time CPT. This is one of the most consequential rules in F-1 work authorization. Keep careful records of your CPT dates and hours.
  • Part-time versus full-time classification matters. 20 hours/week is the line. Working 21 hours/week on CPT is full-time CPT, even if your employer calls it part-time.
  • CPT authorization does not extend by itself. If your authorized period ends and you continue working without a new I-20, you are working without authorization.
  • Graduate students may be eligible from day one if the program explicitly requires immediate practical training (some language programs, some graduate co-op programs). This must be documented in the curriculum.

What this page does not cover

  • On-campus employment — working on campus for your school or an affiliated employer
  • Optional Practical Training (OPT) — the 12-month off-campus training period not tied to a specific course
  • STEM OPT — the 24-month OPT extension for STEM degree holders
  • The 90-day unemployment rule — limits on unemployment during OPT
