When is volunteering legal and when does it count as work?

Genuine unpaid volunteering for public agencies or tax-exempt nonprofits is generally permissible for noncitizens, even without work authorization. But unpaid work for for-profit companies almost always counts as unauthorized employment. The key question is: who primarily benefits from the work?

Applies to: F-1 J-1 H-1B H-4 L-2 General Verified April 21, 2026

Who this applies to

This page is for noncitizens who are considering unpaid volunteer work, internships, externships, or similar arrangements. It applies particularly to people who have limited or no work authorization (such as F-1 students not on OPT, H-4 spouses without an EAD, or people on status that does not permit work) who want to understand whether participation in unpaid activities could constitute unauthorized employment.

What the law says

The United States does not have a simple, bright-line rule separating permissible volunteering from unauthorized employment for immigration purposes. Two bodies of law are relevant: the Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA) and USCIS’s interpretation of “employment.”

The FLSA primary beneficiary test for internships:
Under DOL Fact Sheet #71, an unpaid internship at a for-profit company is lawful under the FLSA only if the intern (not the company) is the “primary beneficiary” of the arrangement. Courts use a seven-factor balancing test:

  1. Is there a clear understanding that the position is unpaid?
  2. Does the internship provide training similar to an educational environment?
  3. Is the experience tied to the intern’s formal education?
  4. Does the internship accommodate the intern’s academic commitments?
  5. Is the internship duration limited to a period when it provides the intern with beneficial learning?
  6. Does the work supplement (rather than displace) the work of paid employees?
  7. Is there no guarantee of a job at the end?

If the intern is the primary beneficiary, the FLSA does not require payment. But even an unpaid internship that meets the FLSA test may still constitute “employment” for immigration purposes — these are separate analyses.

USCIS’s view of unpaid work:
USCIS has stated that unpaid work can constitute “employment” for immigration purposes if the worker provides a service that primarily benefits the employer. The key factors are:

  • For-profit vs. nonprofit/government: Volunteering for a public agency (government entity) or a tax-exempt 501(c)(3) nonprofit in a traditional volunteer capacity is generally permissible, even without work authorization. Volunteering for a for-profit company almost never qualifies.
  • Who benefits: If the work directly benefits the employer’s revenue-generating operations or displaces paid workers, it is likely employment.
  • Compensation in any form: Receiving any compensation — including non-cash benefits like room and board, transportation reimbursement, or gift cards — can convert volunteering into employment.

29 U.S.C. § 203(e)(4) provides that individuals who volunteer their services to a public agency are not “employees” under the FLSA. This FLSA exemption is often cited in discussions of permissible volunteering for immigration purposes, but it is not a direct statement of USCIS policy.

Common gotchas

  • “Unpaid” is not a magic word. Calling something “unpaid” does not automatically make it permissible. The substance of the arrangement matters.
  • For-profit startup volunteering is almost always unauthorized employment. Even if a startup cannot pay you and you genuinely believe in the mission, working for a for-profit company without compensation that displaces regular employees is unauthorized employment for immigration purposes.
  • Shadow experience / observation days. A one-time, short-duration “job shadowing” experience (observing work without performing it) is generally not considered employment. But once you start performing tasks that have economic value to the employer, the line has been crossed.
  • F-1 OPT and volunteering: During OPT, working unpaid for a nonprofit in your field may count as qualifying OPT employment (meeting the “practical training” requirement) — but you must still report it to your DSO. Not all DSOs treat unpaid work as qualifying OPT employment; check with yours.
  • Religious and civic volunteering. Volunteering at a place of worship (regardless of its tax status) or for a civic organization is generally permissible — these are classic volunteer roles that do not displace paid workers.
  • Academic research. Participating in unpaid academic research at a university (as a student or post-doctoral researcher) is complicated. Research that is a core part of your academic program may be covered by your student status. Research outside your program that benefits the university’s commercial activities may be employment.

What this page does not cover

  • Working without authorization — full consequences of unauthorized employment
  • OPT and the 90-day unemployment rule — whether unpaid work counts during OPT
  • CPT — curriculum-linked training that is separate from volunteering
