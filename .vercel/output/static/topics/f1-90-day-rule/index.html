What is the 90-day unemployment rule on OPT?

F-1 students on post-completion OPT may be unemployed for a maximum of 90 cumulative days during the standard 12-month OPT period. STEM OPT adds a separate 60-day allowance. Exceeding either limit violates your F-1 status.

Applies to: F-1 Verified April 21, 2026

Who this applies to

This rule applies to F-1 students on post-completion OPT — the 12-month OPT period that begins after completing all degree requirements. It also applies, with a separate allowance, to students on the 24-month STEM OPT extension.

Pre-completion OPT does not have a defined unemployment clock in the same way, because the student is still enrolled.

What the law says

USCIS policy (as explained in USCIS Policy Manual, Vol. 2, Part F, Ch. 4) provides that:

  • During standard post-completion OPT, a student may accumulate a maximum of 90 cumulative days of unemployment.
  • During the 24-month STEM OPT extension, the student may accumulate up to an additional 60 cumulative days of unemployment — for a total of up to 150 days across the entire 36-month OPT period (including both the standard and extension periods combined).

“Unemployment” means periods when you are not engaged in qualifying OPT employment and not in an approved gap. Days accumulate from your OPT start date (the date on your EAD), regardless of whether you have a job lined up.

What counts as qualifying employment:

  • Full-time work directly related to your major field of study
  • Part-time work (less than 20 hours/week) during pre-completion OPT does count
  • Part-time work during post-completion OPT is generally treated as unemployment unless special circumstances apply — you should confirm with your DSO

Reporting: You must report your employment (start date, employer name, employer address, and supervisor) through the SEVP Portal or through your DSO. Failure to report does not reset the unemployment clock — unreported time still counts as unemployment.

Common gotchas

  • The clock starts on your EAD start date, not when you begin a job. If your EAD is valid from June 1 and you don’t start working until September 1, you’ve already used 92 days — violating your status. Don’t request an early start date if you’re not ready to work.
  • Unpaid internships count as employment — if they genuinely relate to your field and are treated as legitimate training. However, see the volunteering vs. work page — simply calling something “volunteer” or “unpaid” does not automatically make it authorized under OPT.
  • A gap between two jobs adds up fast. If you leave one job and take 45 days before starting the next, and then take another 50-day gap later, you’ve exceeded 90 days.
  • Job searching while unemployed is normal, but time is limited. You can begin a job search, attend interviews, and negotiate offers during the 90-day window — but actually starting work before the window closes is critical.
  • STEM OPT 60-day allowance is separate. The 60 days during STEM OPT do not add to or combine with the 90-day standard OPT allowance in a single bucket. Once you are in STEM OPT, a new 60-day clock tracks that period.
  • There is no appeal mechanism to “reset” the unemployment clock. Once you exceed the limit, you have violated your F-1 status. Options at that point are limited and may require departing the US and re-entering.
  • Authorized leave (medical, maternity/paternity, etc.) may not count as unemployment if properly documented and your DSO is kept informed. The rules here are complex — consult your DSO and consider legal advice.
